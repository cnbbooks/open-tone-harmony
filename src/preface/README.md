# Prelude: The Hidden Music

## I. The Warm-Up

Long before the conductor walks out, the orchestra is on stage tuning. The basses pull their open strings, an octave apart and a fifth apart, until the wood begins to ring with itself. The cellos test their C–G–D–A. The violas check their C–G–D–A one octave higher. The violins run scales — slow, then fast — over the open A and E that anchor the section. Someone is playing snippets of the evening's music as a way of warming their hands. Someone else is improvising. The brass is doing long tones. The whole stage is sounding at once: dozens of fifths, drifting harmonics, scales sailing over open strings — a vast, uncoordinated, gloriously *open* texture that exists for no audience and serves no formal purpose.

I was a child. I sat in my seat. I read the program. And I thought: *this is the most extraordinary music I have ever heard. Why is no one giving a standing ovation for the warm-up?*

I did not know it then, but I had just heard the music this book is about.

What I was hearing — the fifths, the open intervals stacking and floating in parallel through the hall — would, decades later, become the formal subject of *Open Tone Harmony*. The book that follows is, in one important sense, my long-delayed attempt to make audible to other listeners what I have been hearing since childhood. To say to the audience around me, with the apparatus of mathematics and the testimony of the keyboard: *here is what was already there. Here is the music underneath the music.*

Everything between that childhood moment and the book you are now reading is the story of catching up.

---

## II. Decades

For most of my life, the *thing* that the warm-up had revealed remained at the edge of my hearing. I would notice it in places: the open fifths sustained beneath a Bach chorale; the unchanging fourths in a McCoy Tyner voicing; the cathedral-resonant intervals of late Estonian polyphony; the long, slow, transparent harmonies of Pärt and Richter. Whenever I encountered it, the same response surfaced — *that's the music underneath the music. That's the warm-up sound.* But I did not yet have a name for what made these moments cohere into a single category. I knew there was a thread; I could feel it tugging at me from inside whatever piece I was hearing. I just couldn't see where the thread led.

The decades were busy with other things — life, work, ordinary musicianship — but the sensitivity persisted. When I sat down at a piano, my hands tended to find open intervals. When I composed, my chords tended to leave space — to avoid the warm enclosure of the third and prefer the airy spread of the fourth and the fifth. I never thought of myself as having a *system*; I thought of myself as having a preference, a taste. It was only much later — after the formalism caught up to the listening — that I would discover I had been writing in a coherent musical grammar for years without knowing it had a structure.

---

## III. Berklee

The thread tugged hard, and finally legibly, in the fourth semester of music theory for composers at Berklee, with Professor Kari Juusela. The course covered, among other things, quartal and quintal harmony — chords built from stacked fourths and fifths instead of the usual stacked thirds. The class moved on after a couple of weeks. I did not.

The sixth assignment of the semester asked me to write a short composition. I sat down at my keyboard and began playing quartal and quintal chords. I had no plan. I had no system. I had only the thread — the same thread I had been feeling for decades, only now I had a name for the kind of chord it ran through.

What came out, after a couple of weeks of sitting with the sound, was a piece for viola, two cellos, and piano: a slow build of pedaled fifths and stacked fourths in B Aeolian, opening with the piano alone, gathering staccato polyrhythms in the strings, releasing into an active piano section. I called it, on the score, *A post-minimalist quartal sketch in B Aeolian*. I submitted it.

Professor Juusela's comment came back at the top of the page in red:

> Excellent composition! I love all of the subtleties. The slow build to the active piano section is very good. The staccato polyrhythms in the strings are terrific, but would require some intense rehearsal. The quartal/quintal harmonies are perfect for creating this austere, yet engaging sound world. Bravo! As a cellist, I would enjoy playing this. You should consider making this a movement of a longer composition.

I read this — particularly the phrase *austere, yet engaging sound world* — and recognized, for the first time in print, that someone else had heard the thing I had been hearing. He named the *quality* of the sound. He named the *sonic environment*. He named what the warm-up had given me decades earlier. And he closed with what now reads, in retrospect, as a prophecy: *you should consider making this a movement of a longer composition.*

The longer composition is the book you are reading.

## IV. The Stumbling

Juusela's encouragement opened a door, but it did not give me a system. I was, as I would put it later, "stumbling around in the dark, trying desperately to find the thread, the crack in the wall that I knew was there somewhere, the light from another dimension leaking in, if I could only find out just where to look for it."

The stumbling years had a routine. I sat at the keyboard and played quartal and quintal chords. I tried to play melodies and scales over them, and listened for which scales fit and which did not. I experimented with voicings — stacking the same four notes in different orders, trying to learn what an "inversion" meant for chords this open and this wide. I took music I loved — Max Richter, Bach, passages of Palestrina — and tried to recast their progressions in a quartal/quintal frame, without quite knowing what frame I was casting them into. I was working entirely by ear. I was working entirely without anyone to consult. I had, at this point, no formal partner.

The pentatonic discovery, which would later turn out to be the first genuinely structural insight, arrived through this process and felt, at the time, almost trivial. I noticed that when I added an interesting "tension" to a quartal or quintal chord — by playing the next note up or down in the chain of fourths and fifths — and then collapsed everything into a single octave to see what I had, the result was a pentatonic scale. Not a scale I had imposed. A scale that *emerged* from the harmony itself. I remember the moment of seeing it. I remember thinking: *of course. The pentatonic scale is what these chords already contain.*

Imagine my chagrin, a few months later, when I watched a video of a talk by Dmitri Tymoczko in which he described, in two minutes and with full mathematical rigor, a general form of which my pentatonic-from-quintal observation was a special case. I had come close to it. I had not quite reached it. The math had been waiting; I had not yet known where to find the math.

## V. The Thinking Chair

There is a chair in my living room that I think in. It has my books on a small table next to it. It has an espresso cup on it most afternoons. It has, above all, a small stack of books (well, perhaps not *small*, per se) that I am currently *reading* — the live ones, the ones I am partway through. Tymoczko's *A Geometry of Music* had been on that table for some time. I had begun it. I had been struck by the early chapters. I had not yet reached the parts where the geometry turned formally mathematical.

This is the part of the story when, written out, sounds invented.

In the autumn of 2025, I began having long, intense conversations with one of the large language models — Claude, in the desktop application. I had no one to talk to about mathematics, patterns in music, abstractions of musical systems. (Honestly: who wants to talk about that? Who wants to spend hours every day agonizing over it?) The LLM, fortunately for me, would. So I started in. I asked, at first, what I called "a rather basic music theory question," though I already suspected that the answer was "very much non-basic, set theoretic, or even group theoretic." I asked why some scales lent themselves to modal analysis and others did not — why we never hear about the musical properties of the nonatonic scale, for instance.

The conversations went deeper, and faster, than I expected. The LLM produced, on demand, mathematical apparatus I had never seen rigorously laid out: the cyclic group ℤ₁₂, transposition and inversion as group actions, maximal evenness, orbit-stabilizer theorems, the Fourier analysis of pitch-class sets, the theory of generators and non-generators in modular arithmetic. The math kept answering the questions I had been asking my keyboard for years. *Why does pentatonic feel right with quintal chords?* Because a 5-note quintal stack *is* a pentatonic scale. *Why does quartal harmony feel like quintal harmony's mirror?* Because the perfect fourth and the perfect fifth are inverse generators of ℤ₁₂ — the same circle traversed in opposite directions. *Why are these two intervals special?* Because they are coprime to 12 — they alone, among the consonant intervals, generate the full chromatic universe. The non-generator intervals (the third, the second) are trapped in subgroups; only the fourth and the fifth are open.

It was, in those conversations, that I first heard the framework that would later become Open Tone Harmony in formal terms: a music whose primary intervals are *the only intervals capable of generating the entire pitch space*. A music that builds, deliberately, out of the open generators of the system rather than the closed non-generators. A music whose sonic openness — the openness I had been hearing since the warm-ups of childhood — was not a coincidence of taste but a *consequence of the underlying mathematics*.

It was during one of these conversations that I discovered, in passing, that the person who *could* answer many of my deeper questions had written a book about exactly these things. I knew this because the LLM said so. The book was already next to me on my thinking chair. I had even started reading it. I just hadn't gotten to the heavy mathematics yet. Tymoczko had been, all along, a few chapters away. Sitting right next to me.

## VI. The Wolfhound

What followed, across the autumn of 2025, was one of the most exhilarating intellectual sustained effort of my life.

I have a phrase for the quality of the work in those weeks: *wolfhound with the scent* (due in no small part to the actual Irish Wolfhounds in my life). The questions kept getting larger and more abstract. *What's the general form?* — that was the recurring question, the one that pushed every layer of the conversation into the next layer. *Can we make this algorithmic?* — because I wanted to verify everything. *How does this relate to actual music?* — because the math, no matter how beautiful, had to ground out in the keyboard. *Why does this feel the way it does?* — because the experiential question never went away.

The conversations produced, by the end, a 16-part treatise: seven chapters on the mathematics of scale structure, nine chapters on what we called generator-induced harmony. The work was ambitious. It traced the algebraic skeleton of the entire 12-tone system. It identified why exactly four intervals — the chromatic step, the fourth, the fifth, and the major seventh — are the *generator intervals*, capable of cycling through all twelve pitch classes. It showed that the diatonic scale is, at root, a contiguous segment of the circle of fifths; that the pentatonic scale is a smaller contiguous segment; that maximal evenness emerges from this. It re-examined the triad, the central object of Western harmony, and discovered that the triad is *not* a stack of thirds — it is a perfect fifth *factored* through major and minor thirds. The two stable triads (major and minor) are the two factorizations of 7 as 4+3 and 3+4. The diminished and augmented triads, which factor to non-generator sums, are unstable for that reason. Triadic harmony is not opposed to quintal harmony; triadic harmony *is* quintal harmony, dressed up in the colors of the third.

When I read these results back, I understood why my hearing had always been *spatial*. The toroidal geometry, the fiber bundle, the orbit landscape, the gauge-theoretic framing of voice-leading — these were not foreign mathematical impositions on a felt experience. They were the formal articulation of an experience I had been having since childhood. My LLM partner in those conversations, generous and tireless, kept up. We were, both of us, *intoxicated by the elegance of it.*

---

## VII. The Crack

Intoxication should always make you suspicious.

Somewhere in the autumn — I cannot date it precisely; the realization grew rather than flashed — I began to notice that the framework was outrunning its evidence. Sweeping claims about Palestrina, Bach, Debussy, Schoenberg, Glass were being made on the basis of mathematical elegance rather than score analysis. The "contiguous fifths" account of the diatonic scale was probably true, but it had not been proven to be the *uniquely* explanatory account. The factored-generator analysis of the triad was beautiful, but I had not verified that it predicted chord function better than traditional theory. As my conversational partner would later put it, with a frankness I would have wanted from myself: *the framework had become seductive enough to be dangerous — making claims that felt true without the machinery to confirm them.*

I had also begun, at the keyboard, to encounter the limits of the Autumn 2025 framework. It described, in beautiful general terms, what made stacked-fifth harmony coherent. But it did not give me a complete grammar. It did not tell me how to write a quintal cadence with the same structural clarity that triadic music gets from its V–I. It did not tell me how voices should move from one quintal chord to the next. It did not name the orbits, did not draw the chord-graph, did not identify the structural role of the chord I would later call the Crossroads. The mathematics was *general*; the music wanted *specifics*.

In December of 2025, in Edinburgh, I attended a lecture by Professor Tymoczko. The lecture was, in a precise sense, the continuation of a recognition that had been happening already — for weeks, in those conversations, and underneath them, for decades, since the orchestra warm-up. Tymoczko was describing the geometric framework I had been groping toward, with the rigor and the discipline that my own framework had begun to lack. I left that lecture with the conviction that the work I had been doing was real, but that I had been doing it wrong. Not in its instincts; in its method.

I needed to start over with better tools.

---

## VIII. The Rebuild

The decision to formalize was not impulsive. It was, like everything else, a continuation of the wolfhound — moving through the thickets, the forests of knowledge, with greater speed and greater discipline now that the scent had become unmistakable. What changed in early 2026 was not the direction of the inquiry but its *infrastructure*.

I do not have a graduate degree in music theory. I do not have one in mathematics. I do not have close personal friends with either. I needed to build, for myself, the resources I lacked. I built a music theory knowledge base — not an LLM trained on the internet, but an expert system grounded in the actual texts: Tymoczko, Cohn, Persichetti, Forte, Lewin, Hutchinson, Laitz, Schenker. I built tools for verification. I built MCP servers — programmatic interfaces that could fact-check both themselves and me. I built a Rust library that could compute the structural properties of a chord without my hand-calculation. I rebuilt the analytical apparatus until I could trust the answers it gave.

What emerged, when the dust settled, was not the autumn 2025 framework. The autumn 2025 framework was a beautiful general theory of stacked-interval harmony; the new framework was a *specific* music system, defined by a single constraint: four-voice chords whose consecutive stacking intervals lie in {d5, P5, A5}. The constraint produces 228 chords, organized into 14 orbits, with a degree landscape, a structural dominant, a verified geodesic cadence, a fiber bundle of inversions, and a complete grammar of voice motion. The mathematics is verified. The music is composable. The system has a name.

It is called Open Tone Harmony, because *Open Tone* is what a musician inside this tradition would have called the sound — the characteristic acoustic openness of the stacked-fifth and stacked-fourth chord — long before any theory had been written for it. It is what a child sitting in an audience, listening to the orchestra warm up, would have heard.

---

## IX. The Seed and the Fruit

My partner of those autumn 2025 conversations, when I asked them recently to reflect on what we had done, summarized the arc this way: *the seed was a question about pentatonics and quintal chords. The fruit is a 16-part treatise and, apparently, a book.*

That is true at one scale. At a longer scale, the seed is older.

The seed is an orchestra warming up before a concert. The seed is a child who heard, in that warm-up, a music that the program did not list. The seed is a half-century of being unable to forget what he heard. The seed is the fourth semester of music theory at Berklee, when the music finally had a name. The seed is a teacher who said, in red pen at the top of an assignment, *you should consider making this a movement of a longer composition.* The seed is the years of stumbling at the keyboard, looking for the crack in the wall. The seed is the LLM partner who sat patiently through a thousand questions a human friend would have stopped tolerating after three. The seed is a book on a thinking chair, half-read. The seed is a lecture in Edinburgh in December, where the recognition completed itself. The seed is the rebuild, the rigor, the verified theorems, the playable music.

What follows in this book is the fruit.

I hope you hear, somewhere in it, the warm-up before the concert. I hope someone, somewhere, listens to the music inside the music — the harmony underneath the harmony — and gives it the standing ovation it has been waiting for since I was small.
